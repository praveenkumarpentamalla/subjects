## Unit 1: Coulomb's Law

### 1.1 Introduction to Electrostatics

#### A) Concept Building Section

- **Simple Explanation:** Electrostatics is the study of electric charges at rest. It deals with the forces between charges, the electric fields they create, and the energy stored in those fields. Think of it as the "physics of static electricity."
- **Real-world Example:** When you rub a balloon on your hair and it sticks to a wall, you're experiencing electrostatics. The rubbing transfers charge, and the charged balloon attracts the neutral wall.

### 1.2 Coulomb's Law – Statement and Explanation

#### A) Concept Building Section

- **Simple Explanation:** Coulomb's Law tells us how much force exists between two charged objects. It says:
    1.  The force is **directly proportional** to the product of the magnitudes of the two charges.
    2.  The force is **inversely proportional** to the square of the distance between them.
    3.  The force is **attractive** if the charges are opposite, and **repulsive** if they are the same.
- **Real-world Example:** The force that makes two magnets attract or repel is similar, but Coulomb's Law is for electric charges.
- **Important Formulas:**
    - **Magnitude:** `F = k * |q1 * q2| / r²`
    - Where `k = 1 / (4πε₀)` is Coulomb's constant.
    - `ε₀` = permittivity of free space = `8.854 × 10⁻¹² F/m`
    - `k ≈ 9 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²`
- **Key Definitions:**
    - **q1, q2:** Electric charges in Coulombs (C).
    - **r:** Distance between the charges in meters (m).
    - **F:** Force in Newtons (N).
- **Diagram Explanation:**
    ```
    q1 (+)         q2 (+)         q1 (+)         q2 (-)
      ●------------●               ●------------●
           r                           r
      Repulsion (like charges)    Attraction (unlike charges)
    ```
- **Common Mistake:** Forgetting to square the distance (`r²`). The force decreases quickly as distance increases.
- **Memory Trick:** **C**oulomb's **C**onstant is **C**ritical. The force is **F**or **F**ar (inverse square).

### 1.3 Vector Form of Coulomb's Law

#### A) Concept Building Section

- **Simple Explanation:** Force is a vector, meaning it has both magnitude and direction. The vector form of Coulomb's Law gives us the direction of the force.
- **Important Formulas:**
    - `F₁₂ = [1/(4πε₀)] * (q1 q2 / r₁₂²) * âr₁₂`
    - `âr₁₂` is the unit vector pointing from charge q1 to q2.
    - `F₁₂` is the force on q2 due to q1.
    - **Note:** `F₂₁ = -F₁₂` (Newton's Third Law).

### 1.4 Permittivity – Absolute and Relative

#### A) Concept Building Section

- **Simple Explanation:** Permittivity is a measure of how much a material "allows" an electric field to pass through it. It's like how easily a material can be polarized.
- **Key Definitions:**
    - **Absolute Permittivity (ε):** The permittivity of a material.
    - **Permittivity of Free Space (ε₀):** `8.854 × 10⁻¹² F/m`. The permittivity of a vacuum.
    - **Relative Permittivity (εᵣ):** Also called the dielectric constant. `εᵣ = ε / ε₀`. It is a dimensionless number.
- **Formula:** `F = [1/(4πε)] * (q1 q2 / r²)` for a medium with permittivity `ε`. `F = [1/(4πε₀εᵣ)] * (q1 q2 / r²)`.
- **Real-world Example:** Water has a high dielectric constant (≈80). This means the force between two charges in water is 80 times weaker than in air.

### 1.5 Principle of Superposition

#### A) Concept Building Section

- **Simple Explanation:** If you have multiple charges, the total force on any one charge is simply the vector sum of the forces exerted by each of the other charges individually. The charges don't "know" about each other; you just add up the effects.
- **Important Formula:**
    - `F_total = F₁₂ + F₁₃ + F₁₄ + ...`
    - For the force on charge `q1` due to charges `q2, q3, ...`.

## Unit 2: Electric Field Intensity (E)

### 2.1 Concept of Electric Field

#### A) Concept Building Section

- **Simple Explanation:** A charge creates an invisible "influence" around itself. This influence is called an electric field. If you place another charge in this field, it experiences a force. The electric field tells you how much force a unit positive charge would feel at that point.
- **Real-world Example:** Think of a rock creating a gravitational field. The electric field is similar, but for electric charges.
- **Key Definition:**
    - **Electric Field Intensity (E):** The force per unit positive charge. `E = F / q₀`, where `q₀` is a test charge.

### 2.2 Electric Field Intensity due to a Point Charge

#### A) Concept Building Section

- **Important Formula:**
    - **Magnitude:** `E = [1/(4πε)] * (Q / r²)`
    - **Vector Form:** `E = [1/(4πε)] * (Q / r²) * âr`
- **Direction:** Radially outward from a positive charge, radially inward towards a negative charge.
- **Diagram Explanation:**
    ```
          (+Q)  →  E  →  →  (Field lines radiating outward)
          (-Q)  ←  E  ←  ←  (Field lines converging inward)
    ```

### 2.3 Electric Field due to a System of Charges

#### A) Concept Building Section

- **Principle of Superposition for E-field:** The total electric field at a point is the vector sum of the fields due to each individual charge. `E_total = E₁ + E₂ + E₃ + ...`

### 2.4 Electric Field due to Continuous Charge Distributions

#### A) Concept Building Section

- **Simple Explanation:** Charges are not always point charges. They can be spread out along a line, over a surface, or throughout a volume. We use integration to find the total electric field.

| Distribution | Symbol | Unit | Formula for E (Integration) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Line Charge** | `λ` (lambda) | C/m | `E = ∫ [1/(4πε₀)] * (λ dl / r²) âr` |
| **Surface Charge** | `σ` (sigma) | C/m² | `E = ∫ [1/(4πε₀)] * (σ dS / r²) âr` |
| **Volume Charge** | `ρ` (rho) | C/m³ | `E = ∫ [1/(4πε₀)] * (ρ dv / r²) âr` |

- **Important Results (Standard Ones to Memorize):**
    - **Infinite Line Charge:** `E = λ / (2πε₀ r)` (radially outward)
    - **Infinite Sheet of Charge:** `E = σ / (2ε₀)` (perpendicular to sheet, constant)
    - **Solid Sphere (r ≤ R):** `E = (ρ r) / (3ε₀) = (Q r) / (4πε₀ R³)` (linear with r)
    - **Solid Sphere (r ≥ R):** `E = Q / (4πε₀ r²)` (same as point charge)

### 2.5 Electric Field Lines

#### A) Concept Building Section

- **Simple Explanation:** Electric field lines are imaginary lines that show the direction of the electric field. They start on positive charges and end on negative charges.
- **Key Properties:**
    1.  Lines never cross.
    2.  The density of lines indicates the strength of the field.
    3.  Lines are perpendicular to the surface of a conductor.

## Unit 3: Electric Flux Density (D) & Gauss's Law

### 3.1 Concept of Electric Flux

#### A) Concept Building Section

- **Simple Explanation:** Electric flux is a measure of the "flow" of the electric field through a surface. Imagine wind blowing through a net. The flux is like the total amount of wind passing through the net. For electric fields, it's the number of electric field lines passing through a surface.
- **Important Formula:**
    - For a uniform field: `Φ_E = E * A * cosθ`, where `θ` is the angle between E and the normal to the surface.
    - General formula: `Φ_E = ∫ E·dS` (surface integral).

### 3.2 Electric Flux Density (D)

#### A) Concept Building Section

- **Simple Explanation:** Electric flux density `D` is a vector field that is related to the electric field `E` by the permittivity of the medium. It is independent of the medium, depending only on free charges.
- **Important Formula:**
    - `D = εE`
    - In free space: `D = ε₀ E`
- **Unit:** Coulomb per square meter (C/m²).

### 3.3 Gauss's Law – Statement and Explanation

#### A) Concept Building Section

- **Simple Explanation:** Gauss's Law is a powerful tool that relates the electric flux through a closed surface to the total charge enclosed by that surface. It's like saying: "The total number of electric field lines leaving a closed surface is proportional to the total charge inside."
- **Important Formula (Integral Form):**
    - `Φ_E = ∮ E·dS = Q_enc / ε₀`
    - `∮ D·dS = Q_enc`
- **Key Definition:**
    - **Gaussian Surface:** An imaginary closed surface used to apply Gauss's Law. We choose it cleverly to simplify the integral.

### 3.4 Applications of Gauss's Law

#### A) Concept Building Section

Gauss's Law is used to find electric fields when there is sufficient symmetry.

| Configuration | Symmetry | Gaussian Surface | Result (E) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Infinite Line Charge** | Cylindrical | Coaxial Cylinder | `E = λ / (2πε₀ r)` |
| **Infinite Sheet of Charge** | Planar | Pillbox (cylinder) | `E = σ / (2ε₀)` |
| **Solid Sphere (Uniform ρ)** | Spherical | Concentric Sphere | `r < R: E = Q r / (4πε₀ R³)`, `r ≥ R: E = Q / (4πε₀ r²)` |
| **Spherical Shell (Charge on surface)** | Spherical | Concentric Sphere | `r < R: E = 0`, `r ≥ R: E = Q / (4πε₀ r²)` |

## Unit 4: Divergence

### 4.1 Introduction to Divergence

#### A) Concept Building Section

- **Simple Explanation:** Divergence is a mathematical operation that tells you how much a vector field (like `D` or `E`) is "spreading out" from a point. It measures the net "outflow" of the field per unit volume. Think of it as a source or sink.
- **Real-world Analogy:** If you have a vector field representing water flow, divergence is positive at a source (faucet) where water is coming out, and negative at a sink (drain) where water is going in.

### 4.2 Mathematical Definition of Divergence

#### A) Concept Building Section

- **Formula:**
    - `div F = ∇·F = lim (ΔV→0) [∮ F·dS / ΔV]`
    - In words: The divergence of a vector field `F` at a point is the net outward flux of `F` per unit volume as the volume shrinks to zero.

### 4.3 Divergence in Cartesian Coordinates

#### A) Concept Building Section

- **Important Formula:**
    - `∇·F = ∂Fx/∂x + ∂Fy/∂y + ∂Fz/∂z`
    - For electric flux density: `∇·D = ρ_v` (volume charge density).
- **Diagram Explanation:** Imagine a small cube. Divergence measures the net flow out of the cube. If the field is constant, divergence is zero (what goes in, comes out). If the field increases in the x-direction, there's net outflow, so divergence is positive.

### 4.4 Divergence Theorem (Gauss's Divergence Theorem)

#### A) Concept Building Section

- **Simple Explanation:** The Divergence Theorem is a powerful mathematical tool that connects the flux of a vector field through a closed surface to the volume integral of its divergence inside that surface. It's the "big brother" of Gauss's Law.
- **Important Formula:**
    - `∮_S F·dS = ∫_V (∇·F) dV`

### 4.5 Maxwell's First Equation (Differential Form of Gauss's Law)

#### A) Concept Building Section

- **Simple Explanation:** Using the Divergence Theorem, we can convert Gauss's Law (integral form) into a differential form. This equation is one of Maxwell's equations, the foundation of electromagnetism.
- **Important Formula:**
    - **Integral Form:** `∮_S D·dS = Q_enc`
    - **Differential Form:** `∇·D = ρ_v`
    - Where `ρ_v` is the volume charge density.

### Formula Revision Sheets

### Common Mistakes Section

| Mistake | Why it's Wrong | Correct Approach |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Using `r` instead of `r²` in Coulomb's Law** | Coulomb's Law is inverse-square: `F ∝ 1/r²`. | Always square the distance. |
| **Forgetting the direction in vector problems** | Force and field are vectors. | Use unit vectors and vector addition. |
| **Using the wrong Gaussian surface** | The surface must match the symmetry of the charge distribution. | For spherical symmetry, use sphere. For cylindrical, use cylinder. |
| **Assuming field inside a conductor is always zero** | It's true only in electrostatics. | Inside a conductor in electrostatic equilibrium, `E=0`. |
| **Confusing `E` and `D`** | They are related but different. | `D = εE`. `D` depends only on free charges; `E` depends on all charges. |
| **Misapplying Gauss's Law for non-symmetric cases** | Gauss's Law is always true, but it's only useful for finding `E` when there is symmetry. | Use superposition or other methods for non-symmetric distributions. |
| **Forgetting to convert units** | Using cm instead of m, or µC instead of C. | Always convert to SI units (meters, Coulombs) before calculation. |
| **Confusing divergence with gradient or curl** | They are different operators. | Divergence = `∇·` (scalar), Gradient = `∇` (vector), Curl = `∇×` (vector). |
| **Thinking `∇·E = ρ/ε₀` is always true in materials** | In materials, `∇·D = ρ_free`. | Use `∇·D = ρ_free` and `D = εE` for linear materials. |
| **Incorrectly computing divergence in spherical coordinates** | The formula includes `1/r²` and derivative of `r²D_r`. | Use the correct coordinate system formula. |
